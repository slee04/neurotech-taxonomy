# Paper Vision

*What kind of paper do we want to end up with?*

---

## The core argument

Existing neurotechnology taxonomies draw their boundaries using **device conventions** — does it measure neural *activity*? Is it an engineered *artifact*? These filters (neural specificity + temporal dynamics + device form) are inherited from engineering and clinical traditions. They work fine for technical audiences, but they create **blind spots for governance**.

This paper argues that a taxonomy built for governance needs a different organising principle: **what the technology can access, infer, or alter about a person** — grounded in what the brain actually does (its functions and outputs), not what devices happen to measure.

---

## What makes this publishable

1. **A TIPSS-centred scope boundary.** Most taxonomies ask "does it measure neural activity?" We ask "does it create distinctive risks to Trust, Identity, Privacy, Safety, or Security?" This reframing is not just semantic — it changes what's in and out of scope, and therefore what governance frameworks can see.

2. **Exposing the definition–governance mismatch.** Current definitions implicitly stack three filters (neural specificity, activity/function, device form). Filters #2 and #3 are technically conventional, not ethically grounded. Structural brain imaging is excluded because of a temporal convention. Neuropharmacology is excluded because of a device convention. Eye tracking is excluded because it's a "proxy." Yet all three raise TIPSS concerns that overlap significantly with core neurotechnologies. The paper names this mismatch explicitly and offers a principled way to handle it.

3. **A function-first reframing.** Instead of classifying by device modality (electrical, magnetic, optical...), we can classify by *what brain functions are accessed or altered* — attention, decision-making, identity, memory, agency. This connects the taxonomy directly to the things governance actually cares about (autonomy, consent, discrimination, manipulation) rather than requiring a translation step from "this device uses magnetic fields" to "this threatens cognitive liberty."

4. **Structured treatment of borderline cases.** Rather than silently including or excluding contested technologies, the taxonomy explicitly tags them with justification. This enables systematic sensitivity analysis — "what changes if we include eye tracking?" — which is methodologically rigorous and practically useful.

5. **The three-category extension.** Adding "Integrated Systems" (BCIs, neurofeedback, neuroprosthetics) as a distinct category alongside Sensing and Modulation captures technologies with qualitatively different risk profiles: continuous data flow, real-time adaptation, user dependence, closed-loop control.

---

## The intellectual move

The paper makes a specific move that ties all the above together:

> Convention-based definitions serve engineering and clinical communities well. But governance needs to reason about *what a technology can do to a person*, not *how the device works*. A function-first, TIPSS-informed taxonomy makes the bridge from technology to governance concern **structural** rather than ad hoc.

This is not an argument to abandon technical definitions — it's an argument that governance taxonomies need an additional organising layer that current frameworks lack.

---

## What the paper is NOT

- Not a comprehensive review of all neurotechnologies (the database serves that role)
- Not a TIPSS risk assessment (that's the broader NeuroCET-TIPSS project)
- Not a policy proposal or regulatory recommendation
- Not a critique of existing definitions — it builds on them and adds a governance-facing layer

---

## Target shape

A **Perspective or Comment** piece (~2000–4000 words) that:
- Opens with the problem: governance frameworks inherit engineering definitions that weren't designed for governance
- Demonstrates the mismatch with concrete examples (eye tracking excluded by convention but among the most TIPSS-relevant consumer technologies; structural imaging excluded by a temporal convention that has no ethical basis)
- Proposes the function-first reframing as a complementary organising principle
- Presents the taxonomy structure as a worked example (not the full database — the logic)
- Closes with implications for neurotech governance and convergent technology assessment

## Target venue

**Nature Machine Intelligence** (Comment/Perspective) — sits at the AI/neurotech/governance intersection; read by the interdisciplinary community this work is for.

---

## What success looks like

A paper that becomes the reference people cite when they need to justify **what counts as neurotechnology for governance purposes** — and that establishes "function-first, TIPSS-informed taxonomy" as a named, reusable idea.
